The Vojta method in infants: when to start and how the exercises work

Published 10 June 2026 · Author: Mgr. Miroslav Kutín, physiotherapist and Vojta-method teaching therapist

Has your paediatrician or neurologist recommended exercises using the Vojta method and you are not sure what to expect? In this article we answer the questions parents ask most often: when to start therapy, how the first visit and home exercises proceed, why the baby cries during the exercises and when you can expect the first results.

What is the Vojta method?

The Vojta method (reflex locomotion) is a respected physiotherapeutic method discovered by the Czech neurologist Prof. MUDr. Václav Vojta. It makes use of innate movement patterns that can be elicited in every person by activating precisely defined trigger zones in a specific starting position. In infants this makes it possible to influence motor development positively before substitute (non-ideal) movement patterns become fixed.

You can find detailed information about the principle of the method, its indications and Prof. Vojta on our page Vojta Principle – complete information.

When to start the Vojta method with an infant?

As soon as possible after a problem is identified – ideally in the first months of life. The earlier therapy begins, the better the results that can usually be achieved.

An infant's central nervous system is immature and develops most intensively during the first two years. At first the nerve pathways are often only functionally blocked rather than structurally damaged. Early therapy can release them or help create new connections, allowing the child to develop motor skills normally. This does not mean that therapy later no longer makes sense – the Vojta method helps at any age, including adulthood.

Which children is the Vojta method suitable for?

In infancy and childhood we indicate the Vojta method mainly for:

  • asymmetric development (e.g. the child constantly looks to one side – so-called head predilection)
  • delayed (retarded) motor development
  • central coordination disorders (CCD)
  • cerebral palsy (CP)
  • peripheral pareses of the limbs (e.g. brachial plexus palsy)
  • congenital defects of the movement system (hip dysplasia, pes equinovarus)
  • spinal curvature (scoliosis) and other orthopaedic defects
  • disorders of sucking, chewing and swallowing and disorders of respiratory function

How does the first visit work?

At the first visit the physiotherapist first carries out a detailed kinesiological examination – assessing the child's spontaneous movement, postural reactions and primitive reflexes. Based on the examination, they set the goal of therapy, propose a treatment plan and precisely target the therapy.

They then show the parents specific exercises and train them in performing them, because the main part of the therapy takes place at home. Practical information about booking can be found on the For patients page.

What does the exercise itself look like?

The child is placed in a precise starting position (on the tummy, on the back or on the side) and the therapist or a trained parent uses the trigger zones to elicit a movement pattern analogous to the individual movements in the child's development. The child does not consciously exercise anything; its nervous system responds to the stimulus automatically.

Home exercises are usually carried out several times a day in short blocks. The exact frequency, duration and specific variants of the exercises are determined individually by the physiotherapist and adjusted continuously according to the child's development at regular check-ups.

Why does the child cry during the exercises? Does it hurt?

The Vojta method does not hurt. Crying is an expression of displeasure at the unfamiliar activation, not of pain.

Crying during the exercises is a common reaction in infants to unfamiliar and intense activation – a small child has no other way of showing that it dislikes the situation, much like when being dressed or bathed, which it may also not enjoy. Once the exercise is over the baby calms down quickly and eats and sleeps normally.

If you are not sure whether your child is reacting appropriately, always consult your therapist – properly guided therapy does not harm or traumatise the child.

When do results appear?

This depends on the child's age, the severity and nature of the movement disorder and the regularity of home exercises. With milder asymmetries, changes are usually noticeable within a few weeks; with more serious diagnoses the therapy is longer-term. We regularly evaluate progress and adapt the therapy accordingly.

When the method must not be used (contraindications)

  • acute inflammatory or feverish illnesses (temperature above 38 °C)
  • acute watery diarrhoea and intense vomiting
  • 2–7 days after vaccination (depending on the type of vaccination and the child's reaction)

Frequently asked questions

When is the best time to start the Vojta method with an infant?

As soon as possible after a problem is identified, ideally in the first months of life, when the nervous system is most adaptable.

Does the Vojta method hurt?

No. Crying is an expression of displeasure at the unfamiliar activation, not of pain. The child calms down quickly after the exercise.

How often is it practised?

Usually several times a day in short blocks at home; the exact plan is set individually by the physiotherapist.

Do I need a doctor's referral?

You can come for a consultation even without a referral. A doctor's indication is required to begin the actual therapy.
